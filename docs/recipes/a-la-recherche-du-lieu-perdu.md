#  À la recherche du lieu perdu

Chris Klimas  
Developer of Twine

**Category:** Interactive Fiction  
**Class of E-lit:** Twine stories  
**Dish:** an introduction to Twine writing  
**Required ingredients:** 

Twine application, available free from [www.twinery.org](http://www.twinery.org) (or use online)

## Imagination

**Preparation and cooking time:** under an hour  
**Number of servings and serving size:** one Twine story  
**Rating: 🍳** one pan, easy

**From the chef:** People often describe Twine as a tool for creating branching narrative. I certainly have—it's an easy shorthand. But that's not all you can create with it. Here's a recipe for building a **setting** inside Twine: no need for plot or character decisions; just exploration of space this time.

Let's begin by choosing a place that you can't visit anymore. Think of a place you're fond of, but which has changed or perhaps even disappeared. Maybe the restaurant—closed now for years—where you had your first date with the person who became your spouse; or the house where you lived for the first twelve years of your life; or a beach halfway across the world where you felt truly at peace. Whatever you choose, pick a place that holds meaning and memories.

Now, let's draw. You can use a computer for this if you like, but pen and paper will work just fine. Draw a map of the place you've chosen. It doesn't need to be to scale or look pretty. Just capture the place as best you can. Resist the temptation to look things up. This map should reflect how you remember this place more than its objective reality. Add as many things to see and do on your map as you can.

Once you've done that, imagine leading a tour through this place. Draw a star on your map at each stop you'd make. Connect these stars with lines showing the paths between each point. When you're done, the lines should be continuous. In other words, it should be possible to travel from any one star to all the others. There shouldn't be any isolated paths in your map. You may find that intersections emerge as you draw these lines. Add stars where these occur, too.

Give each star a short name, like "Cluttered Living Room" or "Ankle-Deep in the Water." These are the places we're going to create in Twine.  (You can download Twine from www.twinery.org.)

Open a new story in Twine and add a passage for each star on your map. It's helpful if you arrange the passages like you drew them on your map, so that if the Basement is in the lower-right corner of the map you drew, it's also in the lower-right corner of your Twine story.

Once you've done that, edit each passage and recreate the connection lines you drew between places on your map by adding links between the passages.  Links are made in Twine by putting \[\[double brackets\]\] around a word or phrase. Don't add any other text until you've reproduced all the lines you drew with links in Twine. Also, don't forget to make the links two-way: if your Stairwell and Classroom are connected, there should be both a link from Stairwell to Classroom and vice versa.

At this point, the map in Twine should look similar to the map you originally drew, though Twine draws more-or-less the shortest path between each passage. It's time to add text to each passage.

Choose a time for this place to exist in. It could be a specific day, like May 14, 2004, or a general timeframe like summer. Anchor your imagination in a particular time. In each passage, write a paragraph describing what each place looks like: what it would be like for someone who had never been here before to exist in this place and at the time you're imagining. 

Don't add narrative commentary or characters to your places. It's fine to have people in the background if you're describing a place where people normally would be, like a city street, but don't put people in the foreground. Don't add any more links yet, either. Concentrate on describing the place itself. Describe things neutrally, but with detail.

It can be a little exhausting to write so many descriptions in a row. If it helps, start with a few intense, interesting details in each place and loop back and elaborate on each one as feels natural. Use Twine's Test From Here button (in the passage editor dialog) to try out your story as you work. Things will suggest themselves to you as you write.

Once you feel happy with the visual descriptions you've created for each place, loop back through each one and add detail for at least one more sense: sound, smell, touch, or taste. Also, change the links you had added to first establish connections between places to feel fluent with the rest of the text. You could change the text to imperatives like "Enter the auditorium," but another approach is to add, say, a mention of the door to the auditorium to your description and link that directly. 

When you hit a stopping point, let what you've made breathe for a little. Come back to it a day later if you can—an hour, maybe, if you're in a rush. You need fresh eyes.

After taking a break, try walking through what you've created. See what parts of each place feel most interesting or prominent to you. In Twine, turn those things into links and add detail about them in the linked passages. In other words, let visitors look more closely at parts of your places. Let them look under a bed, pick up a book and rifle through its pages, or open your old school locker. In those passages, give them a link back to the main location so they can keep wandering.

There aren't more steps to this recipe, but that doesn't mean you have to stop here. You could keep developing your text–writing is rewriting, after all. Or maybe you could:

* Make time pass with code but don't rush your visitors. Time could pass in your place subtly and slowly, or dramatically and suddenly. It could take place over a languorous afternoon, or have a mechanical lever that changes winter to spring.  
* Hide something in your place but don't turn your place into a scavenger hunt. Think of something particularly meaningful to you and put it in a place that isn't immediately obvious. Don't add an obvious reward for finding it. Just let visitors observe it.  
* Add a person to your place—but not you. That's a little too easy. Think about who you'd find here and what they would be doing. You don't need to allow visitors to talk with them if you don't want.

But finally, think about someone you'd like to share your place with. You don't have to, of course, but it's worth considering. Often in digital media, we think about either not publishing something at all or publishing it to the entire world. But consider: if you could only share this with one person, who would it be? And of course it doesn't need to be just one person. It could be your best friends or your closest family.

The simplest way to share your work is to use the Publish to File option in Twine. This will produce an HTML file that can be emailed, shared using a cloud hosting service, or even published on your website. Many web applications require server software to work, but Twine HTML files can be opened directly in a browser without any other software involved.
