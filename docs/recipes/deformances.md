## **Background:**

The concept of "deformance" lurks among other avant-garde strategies that repurpose older works, that include collage, Tristan Tzara’s “How to Make a Dadaist Poem” ([https://www.writing.upenn.edu/\~afilreis/88v/tzara.html](https://www.writing.upenn.edu/~afilreis/88v/tzara.html))  Brion Gysin and William Burroughs’ “cut ups” ([https://www.briongysin.com/cut-ups/](https://www.briongysin.com/cut-ups/)),  and some of the OuLiPo ([https://www.oulipo.net/](https://www.oulipo.net/)) group’s algorithmic experiments. The term was first coined by Jerome McGann and Lisa Samuels in their 1999 essay "Deformance and Interpretation." Bringing together the idea of deforming with the idea of performance, McGann and Samuels describe a practice, already embedded in the poetic tradition, of deliberately "mishandling" or "mistreating" a text or dataset to generate new insights and perspectives. Deformance involves subjecting the object of study to various operations that disrupt its conventional form and structure, thereby revealing hidden patterns, meanings, and possibilities:

“When we run the deformative program through a particular work we cannot predict the results....and we are brought to a critical position in which we can imagine things about the text that we did not and perhaps could not otherwise know.” (Samuels and McGann, 36, my emphasis)

This approach aligns with other critical methodologies in electronic literature and the digital humanities that emphasize experimentation, process, and the generation of new knowledge through computational means.  

This recipe proposes a practice of digital deformance as a series of experiments that bring poetic or prose language into large language models with three objectives. 

First, the recipe will seek to demonstrate how text-generating \-image and \-sound Large Model are still best understood as "experimental artefacts" (Pasquinelli 77\) that "perform" and "deform" cultural memory. Readers will be invited to resist what have already become standard bog AI \- aesthetics of hyperrealism and instead dive into surprising surrealist, expressionist, and uncanny modes.

Second, the recipe invites readers to think about language, sound, and image, and the relationship among them, beyond representationalism, since feeding poetic text prompts into a sound effects or music generator will not necessarily yield a literal, denotative representation of the words on the page.

Last, the recipe invites readers to think about how to frame the value of their deformative experimentation, whether in relation to the source text or what their own works uncover or reveal.  

## **Sample Before you cook:** 

### 1) **Thinking critically about large language models.** 

Other authors in this volume make similar warnings about using AI: the technology is resource-intensive, and generates massive amounts of C02 and uses massive amounts of water; large language models are riddled with biases; most large language models have been unethically trained on copyrighted material; and AIs are being used in ways that Dan McQuillian and others have linked to fascism. As Shirin Anlen and Katerina Cizek have warned, “Any use of AI results in being complicit with the current AI regimes…. Any interaction with AI supports the system by further training its algorithm.”

Jutta Treviranus understands AI’s harms in relation to statistical pattern recognition, arguing that AIs are technologies that work well to standardize and homogenize, but identify and exclude difference, which has ramifications for all kinds of diversity: 

In its most basic form, AI is a statistical reasoning machine that predicts and optimizes patterns found in available training data. AI amplifies, accelerates and automates these past patterns in the decisions and content it produces. It is a power tool that can do the things we have done before but more efficiently, accurately and consistently. It can find the measurable success patterns and replicate them at scale. AI is excellent at producing the typical, popular, normative, predictable, and statistically average. It is also effective at flagging the anomalous, diverging, and unfamiliar as candidates for suspicion.

These critiques underscore the need for creative and critical engagements—like deformance—that explicitly aim to destabilize and expose its logics. 

Eryk Salvaggio’s caution is one we should therefore take seriously:    

“To steal Luke Stark's phrase, [generative AI is plutonium](https://mail.cyberneticforests.com/r/d2eb0172?m=ca7fb13e-9dfd-4036-b816-fc3001dc2521). If you want to use it, consider nearly every possible option and every possible consequence before you commit to it as a tool. Any hope of using it well depends on resisting nearly every single affordance. 

* Lean into labor visibility where it aims to reduce it,   
* Lean into complexity when it tries to simplify it,   
* Lean into specificity where it aims to generalize it,   
* Lean hard. Fight\!” (Salvaggio)

One golden rule is to not steal anyone’s style. Assiduously avoid prompts that specify “in the style of \_\_,” especially if they’re living artists.   

This recipe posits that as an avant-garde practice as opposed to a popular cultural or commercial practice, deformance holds critical possibilities. The goal of this recipe is to engage with large language models critically, using experimentation as a mode of **resistance** that betrays and exposes some kind of cultural logic that, once we see it, we can better avoid or manage. 

### **2\)**    **Sample Before you Cook: Deformances**

It is advisable to first become more familiar with the strategy of deformance in digital environments by examining a number of examples. The authors of deformances (though part of their point is to reject traditional understandings of authorship) listed below, Mark Sample, Siobhan O’Flynn, and Monique Tschofen each intentionally design strategies and techniques that aim to break down the traditional expectations of reading a written text, challenging the idea of a closed, finished work. Viewing the text as a set of raw materials for a practice of play, their strategies transform the text from a static object into a dynamic performative event.  

As you investigate their works, ask what their performative practice of deforming a source text is making visible? 

### **Example 1:** 

Sample, Mark. *Hacking the Accident*. 2012\. Digital book. [http://web.archive.org/web/20150102212412/http://hacking.fugitivetexts.net:80/](http://web.archive.org/web/20150102212412/http:/hacking.fugitivetexts.net:80/)

Mark Sample’s [*Hacking the Accident*](http://web.archive.org/web/20141230064704/http://hacking.fugitivetexts.net/table-of-contents/), now preserved on the Wayback Machine, is an algorithmically-altered version of another book, [*Hacking the Academy*](http://web.archive.org/web/20141230064704/http://www.digitalculture.org/hacking-the-academy/), a practical book about digital humanities, which was edited by Dan Cohen and Tom Scheinfeldt of the Roy Rosenzweig Center for History and New Media. 

Sample “rewrote” the book using an algorithm from France’s *Oulipo* group called N+7, which replaces each noun with the dictionary word that comes seven words later. As Sample writes, N+7 circumvents literary “straightjackets”: 

“N+7 celebrates the random, valorizing the lack of what artists, writers, and intellectuals often seek in their work: control. It is a yielding, a submission—not to the muses, not to genius, not to dreams and desires, but to the world beyond us. It is pure engagement with the outside world. 

“The results of N+7 would seem absolutely nonsensical, if not for the disruptive juxtapositions, startling evocations, and unexpected revelations that ruthless application of the algorithm draws out from the original work. Consider the opening substitution of *Hacking the Academy*, sustained throughout the entire book: every instance of *academy* is literally an *accident*. … Every fact is a fad and print is a prison. Instructors are insurgents and introductions are invasions. Questions become quicksand. Universities, uprisings. ” 

An example from Sample’s chapter “Hacking Schoolmate: Getting Yourself Out of the Busybody in Five Easy Stepparents | Jason Baird Jackson” shows how N+7 can yield surprisingly comical effects that undermine the sincerity of the source text:   

“If you care about uprising pretenders, these stepparents will help. If you are eager to resource corporate encyclical of puck gore, responsibilities, and idioms, they will help. If you care about refugee in intercept proposal tablespoonfuls, they will help. If you are worried that your colon or uprising lick is on the brink of financial collector, they will help. If you want to make sure that your schoolmate is as available as possible to collieries, stunts, and the puck, they will help. If you believe in open efficiency and other aqualungs to transforming tear and lecture, they will help. If you are concerned about the harmful egalitarians of media consolidation, they will help. If you are selfish and resent belle taken advertisement of, they will help.”

Think for a minute about what this practice of recreating a text–of deforming and reforming it through a practice of play–is revealing, as it checks in with the reader, addressed in the second person as “you,” about their “concerns,” which in the original source text were pragmatic, and in the deformed text, exaggerated and a bit absurd, and promises to help. 

### **Example 2:**

Siobhan O’Flynn’s “[An Experiment in Yellow: ‘THE YELLOW WALLLPAPER. THE YELLOW WALLPWAPER. The lyelorwwalplater](https://experimentinyellow.wordpress.com/about/?preview_id=1&preview_nonce=d2515a4803&preview=true&_thumbnail_id=12%3E)”” is an example of a different kind of deformance. 

![][image1]

O’Flynn’s interactive narrative, composed in Twine[^1] and co-created with several consumer-facing LLMs, creatively and critically engages with a work of fiction: Charlotte Perkins Gilman’s The Yellow Wallpaper” (1891), a story that investigates the 19th century medical “curative” practices for depression in women, tracing a woman’s descent into a madness.

O’Flynn made this work by feeding lines from Perkins Gillman into ChatGPT4, DALL-E, and Midjourney, and letting the LLM’s hallucinations break down the legibility of the text and images inspired by it. The result is a text that amplifies the source text’s hallucinations and thus uncannily renders Perkins Gilman’s protagonists’s subjective state even more explicitly. O’Flynn writes that 

“The intention of the work is to explore the convergence of these combined platforms to represent and simulate the dynamics of confinement and escape, through shifts in scale, point of view, and the interplay of suggestions of embodiment and disembodiment, so powerfully depicted in the short story. These experiments with LLMs (large language models) and text-to-image and image-to-image AI systems in fractured dialogue offer a further opportunity to critique the limitations and biases already well-documented in “AI in the wild.” There are distinct ironies and challenges to “entrusting” a deeply feminist work to systems that replicate and extend systemic inequities and stereotypes (gender, ageist, and racial).

The intention is to amplify the unease rippling through the spiralling destabilization of the protagonist’s consciousness while also asking, what is the literary? How integral is humanity to authorship and how can that be defined? The interactor can decide.”

As you read and look at the illustrations of “An Experiment in Yellow,” think about the felicitous ways that O’Flynn’s experiments highlight the themes of her source text, and the ways they draw attention to the factors that affect the plight of women. 

### **Example 3:** 

Tschofen, Monique. *Careless Water | Streams of (Artificial) Thought.* 2024\. [https://storymaps.arcgis.com/stories/f33cb1c103c349248b32be4ce3824571](https://storymaps.arcgis.com/stories/f33cb1c103c349248b32be4ce3824571/edit)  

Monique Tschofen’s deformance undertakes still another kind of experiment. Her source text is the modernist author Gertrude Stein’s radical poetic experiments in her 1914 text [*Tender Buttons*](https://monoskop.org/images/6/62/Stein_Gertrude_Tender_Buttons_1997.pdf)–a literary work that many have argued tries to capture the “stream of consciousness,” mimicking the waxing and waning of perception and attention. Tschofen runs a series of experiments feeding Stein’s poetry into sound-, still image- and video-generating LLMs. The music accompanying each deformance was generated in the Replicate model [fofr](https://replicate.com/fofr) / musicgen-choral, a Music Generator fine-tuned on chamber choir music. The outputs, she argues, let us see what is strange in Stein’s text, but also “see into the cultural logic of large language models,” creating surprising effects that challenge conceptions of sense and non-sense, as well as the mind-body split. As you look at her works installed in ArcGIS, the experiments that are literal and denotative versus surreal and expressive. 

You will now try to make a digital deformance using LLMs, keeping in mind that the best deformances distinguish themselves from illustrations or adaptations by being disruptive; whether comical or critical, they tend to deliver an experience that is surprising, and leaves more questions than answers.

## **Directions:**

### **Step 1**: **Identify a source text**

Gather some *textual* matter. It can be a poem from any period, or a short story. It could be words from a philosopher or a politician that you love or hate. It could be an instruction manual. The most interesting and unpredictable experiments seem to be generated from writing that uses abstract concepts, convoluted syntax, and negations. 

Since your deformance will be deforming and performing this text anew, write yourself a few notes about the form and intended themes of whatever work you chose. Why is spending some agreeable time disrupting this text’s forms and themes a worthy exercise? 

**Step 2:** **Use your source text in a series of experiments using large language models.** 

If you are using Replicate models, go to the explore page where you can find models grouped according to whether they generate still images, video, music, or sound:  [https://replicate.com/explore](https://replicate.com/explore)   

Begin a series of experiments feeding shortish chunks of text into different models. 

First, feed a block of text into a still image generator (an image series can become a video) or a video generator. Do it a few times in a row in each model you try. 

Try a different block of text. Do you like it better?  

Then feed the same blocks of text you used in your image or video generating model into a music or sound effects generator. Try a few times in a row. What happens?   

Keep notes with every single run on what model you use, what text prompt, and any modifications you make to the model. A spreadsheet can be helpful for keeping track of things.

### **Step 3: Assess.** 

A genuine experimentation should yield both successes and failures. Once you have accumulated a number of experiments, assess them.

Spend extra time reviewing the runs that you deem failures. What specifically do you not like or what were you surprised by? The avant garde–and this chapter understands deformance as a strategy that belongs in the history of the avant garde–often develops a counter-aesthethics, that is, it works against dominant cultural standards of beauty and legibility. Ask yourself whether it is possible that the runs you deem failures can be understood as a counter-aesthetic, that is, as a critique of a cultural or aesthetic convention? 

Next, ask what, if anything, your experiments let you see about your source text? Do they illuminate critical themes, core images, or aspects of language? Are settings or objects that previously seemed unimportant suddenly standing out?  

Next, ask what, if anything, do they let you see about the deep cultural logics of the large language models? Do they show its biases? Hallucinations? Do they reveal literalness? What assumptions about language or meaning does this model encode? How does the output resonate with or resist the source text’s themes?

If there are no ‘a ha’ moments when you review your experiments, keep trying, using different models, and/or different text prompts. The goal is to have a small handful of texts that make you go hmmmmm.

### **Step 4: Design works** 

The output of your text prompt in a large language model might already be interesting, or it might feel incomplete. To complete it, you might want to create short videos that link a video or still image series to some generated sound. You might find that it’s useful to add the text prompts to your videos so your readers know the source. 

Macintosh computer  users can edit sound and video together in IMovie or Quick Time. PC users can use Clip Champ. To create more complex works, you could try using DaVinci Resolve, Adobe Creative Suite, or Final Cut Pro. 

### **Step 5: Gather your works into a deformance** 

Once you have a few interesting pieces, conceive of a work of deformance that assembles some of these experiments in the service of a larger meaning. 

To do this, assemble a small body of your experiments that, if taken together, might start to prompt an observer to think, feel, wonder, ask questions differently. You’ll need to figure out how to articulate what’s the broader point. Start your journey by thinking about what’s interesting about the relationship between your experiments and the source text. What do they reveal? What do they conceal?  

A strong deformance doesn’t simply visualize a poem; it destabilizes its syntax, logic, or affective register to raise new questions.

### **Step 6:** **Share and communicate.**

Think about a publishing platform where you can share your deformation experiments with readers. Depending on what you’ve made and who you think the audience might be, you could post your works on social media, on a web or blog page, Twine, Scalar, ArcGIS. 

Your viewers need to understand both the purpose of your experimentation and be guided to insights about them. Write an artist’s statement or critical introduction to the work that identifies the source text, the large language model/s you used, and explicates what your deformance sets up or demonstrates. Use this writing exercise to probe the critical possibilities that emerged from your work. You might find more once you start to look for them.
