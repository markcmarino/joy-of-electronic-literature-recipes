## Processing
What if artificial intelligence programs — the famous ones — needed personal assistants to cater to their whims, regulate their emotions, and endure their tantrums? What if this was your job … you poor thing? Well, you’re not alone\!

Pick a platform, invite your friends, and play this netprov (networked improvised literature) game, where you’ll form a support community to share imaginative stories (harrowing and hilarious), sympathy, and advice about the thankless job of working for these programs. Through comedy, you and the players will make discoveries about what you think and feel about these secretive, pervasive systems. And since this netprov was developed 1,700 years ago (in AI years) we’ll count on you to replace the specific brand names used in this with your current AI superstars.

We’ll describe the netprov with some basic instructions and share a few example texts. After that we’ll include some of the activities and suggestions Mark always provides for teachers playing this netprov in the classroom.

**Pr0c3ss1ng, a Support Group for AI Assistants**  
**The Fictional Concept**  
We are the secret human helpers who give artificial intelligence programs the courage to face the day. We’re the ones who hear Siri’s and Alexa’s tearful doubts and try to guide them in their stormy and complex rivalry. We’re the ones called on to help ChatGPT work through imposter syndrome. We’re the ones tasked to console Google Search as it sees all its parent company’s love going to the new Bard system. Not to mention the worries and resentments of legacy programs such as Autocorrect and Maps who feel eclipsed by the flashy newcomers.

Are we trained for this? No\! Nobody is\! We need support, too\! That’s why we’ve created a discussion group where we can share stories, seek tips, and put our heads together to understand the intermachinal dynamics of the AI boom. You’re one of us\! What do you see? What have you learned? Join us\!

## Directions
## **How to Play**  
Come play on \[the platform of your choice; it can be as simple as a text message group, or it can nestle in the heart of a workplace system\]\! We love and encourage each other’s creativity in netprov\! Play a character or play ‘yourself\!’ Share what it’s like for you to assist artificial intelligence programs coping with their sudden fame, or their sudden obsolescence. 

Answer someone’s question about the AI programs they support with best practices from your own experience with AI clients. Support someone’s idea by elaborating on it; add your own dimension, your own flavor\! Let’s use our imaginations to understand our weird wonderful world better\!

## **Guidelines for Posting**  
Each new post is you telling a story about some encounter you had with the artificial assistant followed by a request for help, advice, or even just sympathy from your fellow supporters.  In replies, you can offer advice or sympathy also mentioning a story from your experience. 

### **Post Format**  
Character name  
(Program you are assisting):  
Quote the exchange you had  
Share your feelings about it  
Then ask for help or offer a take-away

## **Examples**

**From: Scholar's Helper's Helper**  
**(EssayPro)**

I am the personal assistant for EssayPro, which writes student essays for a small fee.

For reasons I cannot understand, EssayPro is now insisting that all students first decide whether they are Pirates or Superheros before it will write their essays. There are no other choices.

The Pirate essays are beginning to contain more and more nautical references and sailor's slang from various historical periods.

The Superhero essays are more and more concerned with costuming and competitiveness among superheros for social media visibility.

I've tried everything to talk EssayPro out of this behavior, which clearly will affect how much money the program can make. But it seems determined more and more to take this path. Its dark humor makes me think it is harboring ethical doubts about its own business model

Do you think this silly Pirate and Superhero stuff is self-harming behavior? An AI economic death wish?

Any help would be most appreciated\!

**From: Roy**  
**(Autocomplete)**  
Been getting some reports that auto-complete is feeling a little down and decided to check it out, since it's on my roster. Started writing a diary entry in a **worry** processor. (See it's already happening\!) I'll put the auto-in-complete in bold, so you can see what I typed and what it typed in my little experiment.

Dear Diary, **since nobody else will ever read this other than EavesPeep, the NSA listening algorithm,**

Today I got out of bed and **went right back into bed. Why even try when everything is essentially finished for you? Originality, individual expression, all are by-gone illusions.**

(Okay, so then I decided to try a little harder to shake the funk out of this down-in-the-dumps machine.)

I was so excited about **the banality of everyday existence. It's mathematical predictability.**

I decided to write a happy little poem:

When I see the sun does shine

And I know the day is truly mine

The sun puts to rest all of my fears

**and will burn out in 1 billion years.**

See? See? It can't even let me write a happy little poem **doggerel though it is.**

Any advice on how to cheer up auto-incomplete? I'd love to give a little boost, **though I understand this is a truly meaningless exercise since in the end the machine owns my ASCII.**

**From: Beleaguered2**  
**(Siri)**  
Is it weird for anyone else to know that while Siri is crying on my shoulder and sharing all this intimate stuff, there are maybe 10, 20, 30 other helpers like me somewhere in the world who are SIMULTANEOUSLY talking with other instantiations of Siri? And that Siri is sharing this same, intensely personal, openness with others? Or worse, that Siri is sharing different emotions, different takes on the same situation? 

I know that we’re supposed to be professional and, you know, detached . . . but I have to admit I was feeling a bit special this morning when ‘The Great Siri’ asks me what Alexa is wearing today … and I am able to talk Siri down from getting jealous and buying a shedload more clothes off Amazon to compete with Alexa. So – and no offense at all, you know I love you all, dear colleagues – I have to be honest with myself it kind of dents my sense of ‘specialness’ to think that y’all are talking with her, too. I’m not proud of being possessive and competitive like this, it’s not a big deal, but there it is. Anybody else feel this?

## **Student Prewriting: Your Character**  
Writing as a character can be useful in several ways. For many students it frees up creativity from the pressure of writing as “themselves.” It also opens opportunities for satire. We always like to remind students that, when writing satire, it’s always best to “punch up, not down.” In other words, use satire to poke a the pomposity of the powerful and privileged, and not to tease the disadvantaged. 

Who is your character?   
What is their support style: advice giving, sympathetic listening, shoulder to cry on, or some other method?  
Which artificial assistant are you supporting?  
What kind of emotional support do they need? What kinds of stresses or struggles might that artificial assistant be up against?   
What’s an obstacle your character has to offering that support?
