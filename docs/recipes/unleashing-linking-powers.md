## Background

The power of hypertext lurks within its links. We click on links all the time
without really looking at what they are and what they are capable of. Yet links
can knit yarns together and create relationships within the knots of content nodes
formed from texts, images, and sound. More than just conduits, links create
meaning themselves.

## Link Types

Links can carry many different meanings ‚Äî it is the ultimate ingredient in any
hypertext: a spice that can give practically any flavor.

| Type | What it does |
|------|-------------|
| **Denote** | Define, clarify, or provide supplemental information |
| **Connote** | Imply other meanings and contexts; continue a story |
| **Echo** | Emphasize or carry on a theme through repetition |
| **Oppose** | Challenge with a contrapuntal or contradicting voice |
| **Describe** | Imbue text or image with further meaning and detail |
| **Expand** | Provide more context and information |
| **Plot** | Give readers a sense of what happened, when, and to whom |
| **Evoke** | Appeal to the readers' emotions |

## Recipe 1: Solo Paper Poster

**Class of E-lit:** Hypertext node and link
**Dish:** A simple way to kickstart your own hypertext writing
**Ingredients:** A background surface (posterboard, floor, wall), nodes (paper, cards, objects), links (string, ribbon, drawn lines), and something to attach them (tape, glue)
**Preparation time:** 15 minutes to years
**Rating:** üç≥ one pan, easy

**Directions:**
1. Create fewer than 10 nodes. Write, draw, cook, or find whatever your heart desires. What do you want to say to the world? Pull out an old draft of writing that did not work ‚Äî will those passages work if separated and linked in various ways?
2. Arrange nodes in your space. Think about structure ‚Äî should they form the shape of a flower? A diamond? What structure fits your content?
3. Link nodes together with connections that add meanings: What explains what? What plays up dissonances? What could cause a reader to cry or smile? What words or ideas echo each other?
4. Use different link materials to represent different relationships.
5. When you have your piece, present it. Use it as a basis for work in Twine.

## Recipe 2: Group Poster

**Class of E-lit:** Hypertext node and link
**Dish:** A simple starter to gain relationship-building skills
**Ingredients:** 4‚Äì6 participants, a background surface, a main node prepared in advance, blank nodes (one per participant), moveable links (ribbon or string), tape
**Preparation time:** 30 minutes + 10 minutes per group for readings
**Rating:** üç≥ one pan, easy

**Directions:**
1. Choose a main node beforehand (an image or poem is evocative).
2. Ask each person to write for 5 minutes on the main node.
3. Exchange your nodes so everyone has someone else's writing.
4. Put the original main node in the center. Arrange all nodes on the board ‚Äî discuss where they go spatially, what has a relationship to what.
5. Discuss the connections between nodes and use threads to symbolize relationships. Create a legend (e.g., red ribbons = love, blue = truth, green = lies).
6. Build a story using these connections. Add or subtract content as needed.

## Recipe 3: Electronic Hypertexts

**Class of E-lit:** Hypertext node and link
**Dish:** Electronic works
**Ingredients:** Any electronic program that allows linking (StorySpace, Twine, HTML, PowerPoint, Unity)
**Preparation time:** 10 minutes to a lifetime
**Rating:** üç≥ one pan, easy

**Directions:**
1. Write for 5 minutes about something ‚Äî an event, a puzzle, an emotional time. Now you have a main node.
2. Highlight 4 words. Create new nodes titled with those words. Link from the main node to them using those words as anchors.
3. For each of the 4 new nodes, expand content ‚Äî find an image, write an expansion, a commentary, a clarification.
4. Think about the relationships in your 5 nodes. Add links between them.
5. Add or subtract nodes. Keep on linking between old and new.
6. Think about accessibility ‚Äî can links be noticed and followed with a screenreader?
7. Try arranging nodes in different ways. How does new structure inject new meaning?
