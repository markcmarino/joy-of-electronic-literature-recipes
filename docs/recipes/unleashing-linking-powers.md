## **Background: Make your own connections**

As we noted in the tour of my linkyards elsewhere in this cookbook, the power of cooking a hypertext lurks within its links. We click on links all the time without really looking at what they are and what they are capable of. Yet links can knit the yarns together and create relationships within the knots of content “nodes” (formed from texts, images, sound, etc). More than just conduits from one node to the next, links create meaning themselves. Where the author put the link (sometimes also called the “anchor”) on the original node magically swells in importance as that anchor point now carries an action. Where the link goes to now is colored in what has gone before—and in the current node.

## **Sample before you cook:**

Just as eggs can taste differently depending on how they're used in a recipe, links can play different roles in texts depending on how they are used in the text. Links usually appear in different contexts depending on these roles. For example: 

**External Links.** These links connect the piece to a wider world and to your main purpose and audience. For example, if you are writing about peace and want your audience to volunteer for on the ground actions, link to a site for an organization that advocates for world peace. These can be anywhere in a piece. You may want to distinguish them from your writing or you may want to embed them just like regular links to enfold a part of someone else’s story into your own story world. 

**Navigational Links.** Navigational links help readers find their way to different areas in the hypertext. These links guide readers, like covers or page numbers or headings in print books. This is a way to tuck in meanings like sequences of events as you, the author, guide the reader. Think about this overall navigational schema as you create your hypertexts, as this structure also adds its own flavor of meaning to the piece. 

**Functional Links.** Functional links further the plot and entice readers deeper into your story. For example, you can use a link on “meanwhile, back at the ranch” to a node that explains what another character is doing at the same time, or a link on “he sobbed wildly” to a node that examines  a character’s emotional state (or explains why he is so upset), or link on “as you know, the tachyon particle” to give readers information that is obvious to the characters. These links are usually embedded in the node itself, but you can use plot links at the bottom of a node like navigational links (with the name of a character or situation or time).

**Meaning Links.** These connections that imply meaning beyond the actual words or images (often termed "semantic links")  are my favorites as they can weave more meanings and implications than what is explicitly stated in the text or conveyed in images or sound. You can emphasize themes by connecting similar or repeated phrases, play up dissonances by connecting contrasting ideas, and instill emotions by juxtaposing situations. Rice can be sweet or sour, depending on the context--and these semantic links can flavor a reading so that readers can taste completely opposite understandings. For example, if a story links the word **love** from a young couple holding hands to an older couple hugging each other, the reader groks that there is a continuity in love, a commitment to being there. But if the story linked that same word **love** to a bitter divorce, then the reader sees the fraught underpinnings of conflict in that love. Same word, but now that (hyper)text is spiced with different contexts, innuendos, and totally divergent readings.

There are so many ways that meaning bends--it is the ultimate ingredient in any hypertext: a spice that can give practically any flavor. I'll give a few of my favorite uses in this recipe section—cook to your tastes. You can define things as a straight connection (think Wikipedia or the news); slyly connote something (imagine  gossip or innuendo); further a plot (explore meanwhile, back at the ranch… or here is why Jimmy is afraid of peanut butter); emphasize or echo ideas (bring out what is hidden or repeat what is important); deny or oppose (hold out the other hand, see the other points of view); describe and expand (get the summary and link to the details); and even evoke an emotion (select the sad face to explore depression), Sample Link Dish

Creating a poster is a quick exercise to demonstrate these relationships, and this poster link recipe can be used anywhere with just simple ingredients (paper, sticks, stones, etc.—no computers required.). I have created a sample to explain a few of these possible relationships. I’m sure you can find many more themes here. A bit of a warning, though: while you can connect completely random and unrelated materials, links, like spices, do not themselves make up the entire dish. You need content, and the more related the content, the stronger the links. For example, in this piece, we have Maya, a background event, and a woven philosophy on the same subject. And of course you can find the similarities in the images that resonate with the text as well.

# **Cooking Your Own Connections**

# **Recipe 1: Solo Paper Poster**

**Class of E-lit:** Hypertext node and link  
**Dish:** A simple way to kickstart your own hypertext writing   
**Required ingredients:** *A background (such as a pan, posterboard, floor, wood, walls, car hood, or any unmoving surface), nodes (like cookies, paper, cut up magazines, stones, or any other object for content), and links (you could untangle limp spaghetti; or draw lines on the background; or use ribbons, string, long paper strips, sticks; or find any other way to connect the nodes). For permanent structures, find a way to attach the nodes and links to the background (glue, tape, nails, etc.)*  
**Preparation and cooking time:** 15 minutes to years.   
**Number of servings and serving size:** As much as your ingredients and imagination will allow. 

**Rating: 🍳 one pan, easy**

**From the chef:** *This is a great exercise for kids or students to do together as a writing exercise to grok the power of links. I had done this for myself for Disappearing Rain* ([http://www.deenalarsen.net/rain](http://www.deenalarsen.net/rain))*, and my living room and kitchen walls were a mosaic embroidery thread that lasted through two cats. My mother had just watched* A Beautiful Mind, *about an economist who had created elaborate paper posters in his bedroom--and voiced her concerns that I might um… need to see someone about the mess on the walls.* 

## **Directions:** 

Create nodes (keep it manageable and less than 10 nodes). Write, draw, cook, find whatever your heart desires. What do you want to say to the world? If you can not think of something, then get an image and just free write. Or pull out an old draft of writing that did not work--will those passages work if they are separated and then linked together in various ways? Use your imagination\!

Arrange these content nodes in your space. Think about structure--should they form an image like a flower? A shape like a diamond? What structure fits your content? 

Link these nodes together with connections that add meanings:

* What can be a denotative or informative link? What explains what? What gives more details?  
* What can constitute a plot or time or character link? What are the story elements?  
* What links could play up the emotions and cause a reader to cry or smile or get angry?  
* What words or ideas play off of each other to create interesting connotations, assumptions, unsaid but implied meanings?  
* Where are similar themes? What could be emphasized with echoes and repetitions?   
* Where are opposing ideas? Where can we play up contrasts?

You could use different link materials to represent different relationships.  Feel free to substitute, re-write, re-form your piece based on these possible links.

When you have your piece, present it. You can also use that as a basis for a work--try Twine[^1] where you can create new nodes and links.

# **Recipe 2: Group Poster**

**Class of E-lit:** Hypertext node and link  
**Dish:** A simple starter to gain relationship building skills  
**Required ingredients:** *Participants (4 to 6 per group), a background (keep it simple with what you have: a floor, blackboard, posterboard), a main node (prepare something ahead of time, like an image or a poem or an object), blank nodes to write on (paper is probably best, but any object can be substituted, and keep it to one node per participant), and links (as people will change links, use something that can be moved like ribbons or string). As this is usually done in a school or conference, use tape to quickly create your permanent work.*  
**Preparation and cooking time:** 30 minutes but do allow time for readings )10  minutes per group)

**Number of servings and serving size:** As much as your ingredients and imagination will allow. 

**Rating: 🍳 one pan, easy**

**From the chef:** *This works with any age group and any ability level. I did this at a much simpler level for children with developmental disabilities, and that helped them understand cause and effect, before and after, and other types of connections. Of course, Josh's contribution of actual fish and chips did need to be replaced with a photo. . . I also worked with writers at Brown and we used those examples to start projects in the Cave. So this recipe is a seriously useful introduction to hypertext concepts, and can be done anywhere, any time, with practically anything. Keep it in your back pocket for those impossible classes or groups.* 

This can be done in a class or as a writing prompt. You will need the same ingredients as your own poster (a posterboard, paper, glue, and connective tissue), but feel free to add whatever your group wants as well. 

1. Choose a main node beforehand (bananas are fun, but messy. Images are evocative, and poems can be scintillating for writers to distill amazing senses).

2. Ask each person to write on paper for 5 minutes on the main node.

3. Each response to the main node is now a node. Exchange your nodes, so everyone ends up with someone else’s writing. 

4. Put the original main node in the center of the background. (Sticky putty works best to attach a banana to a posterboard.) Then arrange all of the nodes on the board. Discuss where they "go"—what has a spatial relationship to what. Maybe two pieces are opposite, so they go on opposite sides of the posterboard. Maybe two pieces are connected and they go on top of each other, maybe you need to tear a paper apart or copy it to use in two different places...use your imagination.

5. Discuss types of connections are between these papers and use threads, etc to symbolize these connections. Create a legend to explain these connections (for example, red ribbons are love, blue ribbons are truth, green are lies, and yellow ones are delicious together).

6. Build a story using these connections. You can add or subtract content by taking away or adding pages.

# **Recipe 3 and Beyond: Electronic Hypertexts**

**Class of E-lit:** Hypertext node and link  
**Dish:** Electronic works  
**Required ingredients:** *Any electronic program that will allow you to link texts, (StorySpace, Tinder, HTML, Twine, Powerpoint, Unity, Scroll Cinema, etc.)*   
**Preparation and cooking time:** 10 minutes to a lifetime.  
**Number of servings and serving size:** As much as your imagination will allow. 

**Rating: 🍳 one pan, easy**

**From the chef:** *Doing several small poster hypertexts will help you develop the linking skills to create a larger hypertext on your own—with meaningful links that stitch your texts, images, and sounds together. Here is a recipe to start creating your own work.*

1. Write about something--an event, a puzzle that has been bothering you, an emotional time, a person, etc. for 5 minutes. Now you have a main node.

2. Highlight 4 words in your main node. Create new nodes with these 4 words as titles for those new nodes. Link from your main node to these new nodes, using those 4 words as anchors. 

3. For each of these 4 new nodes, expand your content. Either find an image that you can associate with the word (and its context in your main node) or write about that word. This could be a related story, an expanded definition, a clarification, a commentary, etc.

4. Think about the relationships in your 5 nodes. You can ask the same questions as in the poster recipe. Add in links between your nod

5. Add or subtract nodes. Keep on linking between the old and the new nodes. What does each new relationship add to the overall piece?

6. Think about accessibility—can your links be noticed and followed in multiple modes for readers who may have vision or movement barriers? Can your work be perused with a screenreader? With an adaptable mouse?

7. Try arranging your nodes in different ways. How does forming a new structure or even creating more nodes and links inject new meaning?
