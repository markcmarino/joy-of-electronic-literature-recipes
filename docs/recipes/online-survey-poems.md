## **Background: Multiple-Choice Poetry** 

Not all great meals are necessarily cooked from scratch. Sometimes, adding some creative seasoning to the cheapest store-bought pasta sauce can completely change a dish. Why not apply the same logic to making electronic literature? This recipe will teach you how to turn tedious online surveys into literary works that repurpose user interactivity to create new poetic or narrative experiences. Why reinvent something when we can repurpose tools we already know how to use? Think about this recipe as the perfect blend of high and low: a readymade dish that’s a cross between Duchamp’s *Fountain* and a box of Hot Pockets.

Like surveys themselves, repurposing the multiple-choice genre to create poetry or narrative prose is not particularly new—think about John Asbery’s poetry collection *100 Multiple-Choice Questions* (2000) or Alejandro Zambra’s novel *Multiple Choice* (2016). In the field of electronic literature, we can find successful examples of taking this multiple-choice logic *online* with the repurposing of common digital survey platforms for the construction of literary objects too. Some well-known examples include *All Your Time-Tossed Selves,* an interactive narrative about the destruction of a mysterious city by prolific IF writer Porpentine Charity Heartscape, built on Google Forms. In this poetic narrative, the reader’s plan for leaving the city before it succumbs to natural disaster is determined by their multiple-choice selection of actions (e.g.: Is the city burning or freezing? What will the “knights of the city” do to help? What will *you* take on your escape?) even if the text’s final message about the inevitable final doom is reinforced by the circular structure of the narrative, where different paths are linked to reveal similar options for the reader. In the end, the destruction of the city is “only a symptom of the end of everything,” and Porpentine’s work teaches us to accept the finite nature of life, while encouraging us to carry on, in looping fashion. 

A more poetic example, my own *Room \#1,* also built on commercially available Google Forms, engages the reflective nature of the questionnaire form itself by turning readers to the lack of transparency of the process of online surveying, among many other invisible processes that control our digital life. In the end, an online survey like mine or Porpentine’s, is but another form of cloud surveillance because the data that any reader provides will be stored on a third-party server, where this data could be used to gather personal information or online behaviour that, even if seemingly anonymous, could be eventually used for market research and so on. 

While it is not hard to create interactive literary works in a hypertext fashion in these survey platforms that are explicitly built to connect user actions to new pages in a somewhat repetitive manner, the multiple-choice genre further links interactivity with commercial or bureaucratic pursuits, adding a new layer of symbolism to the genre *and* the platform. What if the environmental destruction of Porpentine’s city had something to do with the uncontrolled development of big tech? What if both our civic and disobedient actions were limited to picking from a drop-down menu? What if the invisible surveillance that hides behind online surveys pointed to other kinds of invisible digital infrastructures that shape our existence *inside* and *outside* the platform? What if corporate rhetoric hid and powered something *within the online survey form* *itself*? Using commercial technology to reflect on these meta-questions could be a powerful way to find out the answer. And while this is a particular invitation for you to consider the specific ideologies and potential impacts behind survey technology and the rhetorics behind Google Forms, you may consider reflecting on the possible implications of your use of *any* corporate technology. 

In this day and age of ubiquitous computing, expansive digital surveillance, pervasive online ratings and all-encompassing data collection, artistic interventions into all-too-familiar tools like online surveys (and their corporate languages) can help us demystify them. Reframing them as tools for our participation can help us identify the limits of our user actions and further expose certain hidden functionalities that allow survey tools to collect undisclosed data about their users. 

We’ve all encountered corporate platforms that force us to behave in one way or another, say one thing or another, choose one thing or another, collect one thing or another… so why not make them work in our favor for once?

# Multiple-Choice Poetry: 

# Recipe: Online Survey Poems

**From the chef**: *There can be as many survey poems as choices in a multiple-choice survey. This example is based on one possible poem I wrote using Google Forms, a very accessible and intuitive platform, “free” for anyone with a Google account. Like every survey of this kind, the project is composed of a questionnaire part and the collected responses. Be aware that whatever you build on Google will be stored by Google. You will retain ownership of any intellectual property, but you will be outsourcing the storing and the managing of your data within their platform. Google will not own the data and will not have the right to use it without your consent, but as any business, Google will have to comply with the necessary authorities that request access to it. Google could also use whatever you store in their servers for the training and development of their products. This, as far as Google’s rights go ([https://transparency.google/our-policies/product-terms/google-forms/](https://transparency.google/our-policies/product-terms/google-forms/)). What you decide to do with your collected responses is entirely up to you. You may as well choose to do nothing but be aware that online surveying will necessarily gather data.* 

## Directions

1. Create your form: Log in to your account and open a “Blank Form”. You could also select from a template (e.g.: customer feedback, RSVP, party invite, etc.,) and modify it according to your needs. Keep in mind that a survey poem is not about rhyming—it’s about using questions, sections, and even the limitations of the form itself to evoke emotion and/or tell a poetic story.   
2. Title and describe your poem. Give your form a title, which may act as your poem’s first verse, and use the “Form description” field as your opening stanza. Good subjects for a survey poem are those that can be explored through a series of questions or that lend themselves to being explored from different angles. For example, you could write about a memory or a personal story and imagine alternative scenarios (e.g., survey questions could be about details or feelings associated with that memory, and different sections could offer alternative futures or different versions of the same story); an emotion (e.g, multiple choice options could describe different facets of a feeling or a situation like joy, grief, dread or frustration, emphasizing the complexity of our human experience); or explore an abstract idea (e.g., ask readers to define a multifaceted concept like “land,” “love,” or “change” in their own words, and build upon its many different connotations). The goal is to reframe the form from a data-gathering tool to a poetic medium, playing with the relationship between the chef/poet (you) and the reader/dinner guest (your audience).  
3. Craft your verses: Before you begin, it’s helpful to understand the basic components of a Google Form and how you can use them as poetic tools.   
   1. *Questions:* Each question can be a verse, or even a full stanza.  
   2. *Answers:* The options you provide for multiple choice, checkboxes, or a linear scale can function as poetic verses or stanzas as well.  
   3. *Text Fields:* Open-ended questions allow the reader to become a co-author of your poem.  
   4. *Sections:* These act as new pages or stanzas, breaking your poem into distinct parts and guiding the reader’s progression.

   You can mix and match different types of survey questions to create poetic effects. Use multiple-choice questions where each option is a different verse or a different path for the reader to take. Use a short answer or paragraph to invite the reader to write their own verses. Use a linear scale to make readers reflect on a certain spectrum. Break up your stanzas using the “Add a section” function to create a new page displaying a new stanza. This is a good way to control the flow of your poem and reveal new parts as the reader moves through different pages.  

4. From here on, the limit is your own inclination to break the rules. Challenge your reader’s expectations by:   
   1. Upsetting grammatical categories by splitting up a sentence between multiple options: separate subject and predicate as different options; write a full sentence, a pronoun, a noun, a description of sorts. Mixing up categorization in this way will also make the collected data useless under certain analytics, which may make things even more fun for you\!  
   2. Playing with formatting. While you can’t create completely custom themes, you may use bold, italics and underlining, as well as certain color options to emphasize specific words or phrases. Go to “theme” on the top right corner of the form.   
   3. Adding your own header images, GIFs and videos to your form. Imagine creating a moving image that made it look like something within the form was breaking\!   
   4. Pushing display capacities even further by making things feel uncomfortable or out of place*.* Try exceeding the dimensions of a regular display screen by writing a very long sentence to make a reader zoom out to reveal a hidden verse.  
5. End your poem. Go to “Settings” to add a final “Confirmation message.” You can decide how to share your poem (embed, link, send email…) as well as  allow readers to submit further responses, emphasizing the recursive nature of some of the themes in your poem, for example, or give them the chance to view the results summary, reinforcing once more that this poem was a survey and that their reading pleasure has now been recorded, has become corporate data, and will turn into kibble for the Google monster. Enjoy.

**Variations:** 

A survey poem can be as simple or as complex as you like.

* Easy: Stick to a single-page poem using only multiple-choice and short-answer questions.   
* Medium: Use multiple sections to create a narrative arc, and experiment with images and links.   
* Advanced: Incorporate conditional logic (“Go to section based on answer”) to create a choose-your-own-adventure poem, or use extremely long verses to play with display limits, or add multimedia elements like audio files to add ambiance.
